Slalom Sales Enablement Tool

October–December 2018

Slalom is a business and technology consulting firm. Clients often don’t know about Slalom or what services they offer. Currently, sales executives are having difficulty with the traditional sales approach of presenting information in the form of PowerPoint decks to potential clients.

1

Project overview

Replace the linear sales pitch with an interactive presentation tool that fosters an organic approach to sales

Goals

Humanize the traditional sales approach

Replace the traditional sales pitch with a tool that will allow sales executives to engage with clients organically.

Enable dynamic storytelling

Allow sales executives to dynamically tell stories instead of the traditional linear format of slide decks.

Raise awareness and establish a presence for potential clients at Google

Raise awareness and visibility to Slalom’s unique relationship with Google and the services they provide for potential clients.

Design core features to be client agnostic

Design the core features and experience to be client agnostic so the tool can be replicated to different clients in the future.

Our success metrics

Qualitative data

Is the tool having success where the traditional approach to sales would have failed?

Are sales executives having success using the tool to sell or raise awareness to clients, where the traditional sales approach would have failed them? Is there a noticeable impact?

Quantitative data

Increase in sales or prospects

Has there been an increase in sales or prospects directly due to the tool? Can we measure this?

Why was this a problem to begin with?

Client resistance

Employees of clients have a resistance towards accepting sales meetings or being approached with a sales deck.

Linear storytelling

The linear format of a sales deck makes it difficult to have organic conversations while navigating content.

Missed opportunities

There are missed opportunities by not being able to present material to potential clients in “parking lot” conversations or casual environments.

Inconsistent narrative

Information was not centralized and instead was divided, and the way the narrative was presented was inconsistent amongst sales executives.

Roles and responsibilities

The Experience Design practice formed a pod of three members: a UX analyst, a project manager, and myself as the product designer. We partnered with another group of three from the sales team. I co-led product strategy, user research, wireframing, visual design, and prototyping while taking ownership of development and the launch of the functional prototype.

Our target users

Primary user

Sales executives in the field

The primary users are the ones having difficulty showing material to potential clients. We need to think about their needs from the lens of a sales executive.

  • How do sales executives work in the field?
  • How can we establish a relationship quickly with potential clients?
  • What are client agnostic problems sales executives face?
  • What are client specific problems sales executives face at Google?
Secondary user

Potential clients and other users who will interact with the tool

These are the people who will be presented information, usually for the first time. We need to think about their needs from the lens of a viewer.

  • How can we humanize the experience between sales and clients?
  • How would they like to be approached?
  • How can we keep them engaged?

Project constraints

We did not have access to sales executives at clients other than Google. Due to this constraint, we couldn't directly assess what commonalities sales executives had between different clients. We had to keep this in mind when designing solutions for the sales enablement tool to mitigate problems if we decided to scale the tool to other clients.

2

Research

What’s the problem with the traditional approach to sales?

We started our discovery by identifying pain points and areas of opportunity with the traditional approach to sales. We went through an exercise with the sales team where we mapped out together what a day in the life of a sales executive looks like using the current approach to sales, and what a dream scenario might look like if they had a perfect tool they could use today.

“Day in the Life” Dream State scenario

Scoping the project

From the pain points and areas of opportunity we co-created with the sales executives, we broke down the project into three phases: proof of concept, functional prototype, and future state.

We would commit resources to the proof of concept and functional prototype. If the prototype is a success, we would determine how we could continue the future state of the project.

3

Our design process

Taking an iterative approach to design

We created lo-fi wireframes in Sketch and made interactive prototypes using InVision to quickly test and iterate based off of real user feedback with our team of sales executives.

Lo-fi clickable prototype

For the lo-fi prototype, the intent was to validate an experience that would trump the traditional approach to sales. Attempting to show a potential client a sales deck is a completely different approach than being able to casually show someone a picture or article through your phone.

For sales executives to use this tool as a conversational tool, we had to determine how to structure content in such a way that allowed for dynamic storytelling to replace the linear format of slide decks.

Hi-fi clickable prototype

For the hi-fi prototype, we continued to polish the experience based off of user testing. There was now a request to tailor the visuals to Google. With the possibility that this tool will scale to other clients in the future, we had to keep in mind solutions that were client agnostic versus those specific to Google.

With the hi-fi clickable prototype created, we waited to hear the results from one of our sales executives using the hi-fi prototype in the wild.

Functional prototype

The proof of concept was a major success. Based on the feedback we heard, it seemed that we were onto something. Our sales executives were itching to be able to have the fully functional experience.

After the proof of concept was completed, our resources from the Experience Design practice were spreading thin. Our UX analyst started a new engagement and we had to switch project managers a number of times.

From this point, I was asked if I could take ownership to design and develop a functional prototype that could meet our MVP goals. We focused our efforts on creating a clickable prototype for a tablet device, so we did not have the full responsive experience in place. I stepped up and took on the challenge to design and develop mobile and desktop experiences while launching the functional prototype for sales to use.

Solutions

Enabling sales executives by creating a tool for a new approach to sales and providing a path to a future state

We created lo-fi wireframes in Sketch and made interactive prototypes using InVision to quickly test and iterate based off of real user feedback with our team of sales executives.

Ability to present material casually on all devices

Background

Imagine you are in the parking lot having an interesting conversation with some at work about solving a problem. They happen to be a sales executive and next thing you know they are trying to show you slide deck on their laptop.

No one wants to see a sales deck.


Solution

By designing a responsive website experience, we enabled our sales executives to display material on all devices, casually. The ability to frame the situation for the potential client from viewing a presentation to having a conversation was powerful.

People are willing to look at an article on your phone and have a quick conversation.

Structure content and create navigation methods to dynamically tell stories

Background

Sales executives had difficulty showcasing all the projects Slalom had completed for Google from a holistic standpoint. There was no formula for highlighting past work we’ve done with clients. With inconsistent messaging, the narratives being presented could vary or be incorrect.


Solution

We created an interactive project timeline to show projects in a format to help sales navigate quickly amongst projects while telling the overall story.

We also created a template for project case studies to present information in a consistent manner, highlighting project overview, business challenges and the results provided.

Designing the core features to scale

Background

Google is just one of many clients. With the success of this project, there was a request to potentially scale this tool to many users


Solution

By separating the client specific needs from the ones that are client agnostic, we were able to design the core features in a way that were agnostic in terms of design or content.

5

Conclusion

What I learned from this experience

We take the ability to create digital products for granted sometimes. By creating a tool to replace the traditional sales approach, Slalom sales executives have had real-world results.

There have been stories of Slalom sales executive using the tool in action in parking-lot situations almost daily. There are even accounts of sales executives saying that they landed a gig because the tool spoke directly to the client and quickly addressed a need.

I had some doubts that we might have been over-engineering an experience, when we could have used other interactive presentation software and save some of our Experience Design practice resources, but the results don’t lie. The new businesses opportunities being granted out-weigh the effort from our end.


Next steps

With such wild success with the sales enablement tool, we decided to invest further resources into creating a scalable version of this tool. In the future, Slalom will be able to spin up tailored web experiences for any one of their clients.
